The rapid growth of industrial automation, real-time monitoring, and the Internet of Things (IoT) has generated an urgent need for optical access networks that can simultaneously support ultra-dense connectivity, high data throughput, and deterministic low latency. As factories, utilities, and transportation systems increasingly rely on machine-type communications (MTC) and distributed control systems, optical networks must accommodate thou-sands of endpoints while maintaining precise synchronization and energy efficiency—key requirements for indus-trial-grade reliability. Conventional passive optical networks (PONs), despite their extensive deployment and tech-nical maturity, face fundamental scalability barriers in such ultra-dense scenarios. These limitations primarily stem from three critical aspects: the high cost of wavelength-specific transceivers, stringent optical power budgets under high split ratios, and the complexity of carrier recovery in coherent detection systems [1,2,3].
To overcome these constraints, recent studies have explored advanced PON architectures integrating or-thogonal frequency division multiplexing (OFDM), multiwavelength generation, and coherent detection technolo-gies. OFDM-based PONs offer high spectral efficiency, strong resilience to chromatic dispersion (CD) and polariza-tion-mode dispersion (PMD) , making them promising candidates for next-generation industrial optical access networks[4,5]. Critically, OFDM provides inherent latency advantages through parallel subcarrier multiplexing. In conventional time-division multiplexing (TDM) systems, users must queue for allocated time slots, which introduc-es variable and unpredictable delays. In contrast, OFDM enables simultaneous transmission across multiple subcar-riers, allowing immediate data access without slot-based contention. This deterministic low-latency characteristic is particularly crucial for industrial automation scenarios requiring real-time control and synchronization, where even millisecond-level delays can compromise system stability and safety.
However, practical implementation of multiwavelength OFDM-PONs for ultra-dense connectivity remains challenging. To support massive user access while maintaining cost-effectiveness, a large number of optical carriers with precise frequency spacing are required. Optical frequency combs (OFCs) have emerged as an enabling solution, capable of generating numerous wavelength channels from a single compact source [6,7]. Unlike conventional laser arrays that require individual wavelength control and stabilization, OFCs inherently provide equidistant carriers with stable frequency relationships, significantly reducing system complexity and cost for large-scale deployments [8,9]. This multiwavelength generation capability directly addresses the scalability requirements of ultra-dense ac-cess networks, enabling support for thousands of endpoints without proportionally increasing hardware complexity. Nevertheless, efficient carrier regeneration at the optical network unit (ONU) remains a critical challenge that must be addressed to realize practical ultra-dense access networks.
To enable cost-effective carrier recovery at the ONU, injection-locked lasers (ILLs) have been proposed as low-cost alternatives to external cavity lasers (ECLs) [10,11]. ILLs leverage optical injection locking to regenerate the transmitted carrier locally. This eliminates the need for independent laser sources at each ONU. Critically, the injection locking process preserves the phase coherence of the transmitted carrier [12,13]. This enables coherent detection at the ONU without complex carrier recovery algorithms. Our recent work systematically investigated ILL-based coherent reception [14,15]. We demonstrated polarization-independent operation through fading-resistant carrier design and Alamouti-coded signal processing. This approach effectively addresses polarization dependence in practical deployments while maintaining stable injection locking. ILLs offer locking ranges exceeding 300 MHz at practical injection power levels. These characteristics make ILLs suitable for regenerating phase-coherent carriers in multiwavelength access networks.	
In this work, we propose and experimentally validate a large-capacity and massive-connectivity polariza-tion–carrier dual-reuse CO-OFDM-PON architecture enabled by OFC sources. In this design, downstream data and carrier signals are mapped onto orthogonal polarization states, allowing the carrier to be reused for both down-stream coherent detection and upstream transmission—with upstream carrier regeneration achieved via ILLs at the ONU. This dual-reuse strategy not only significantly reduces the number of required laser sources and enhances system scalability but also maintains high transmission quality without compromising latency performance. To ensure reliable operation under high split ratios and multiwavelength conditions, key enabling subsystems (includ-ing the OFC source, ILL, and pre-amplified EDFA) are comprehensively characterized and optimized.
This paper's main contributions encompass three interconnected aspects: First, it designs and experimentally demonstrates a polarization–carrier dual-reuse architecture that leverages OFC-based multiwavelength generation, directly addressing the cost and scalability limitations of conventional OFDM-PONs in ultra-dense scenarios. Sec-ond, it conducts systematic analysis of subsystem performance (OFC, ILL, EDFA) and fiber nonlinearities, quanti-fying their specific impacts on system scalability to provide actionable insights for industrial deployment. Third, it experimentally demonstrates 16×100 Gb/s 16-QAM OFDM transmission with support for up to 4096 nodes, verify-ing the architecture's practical suitability for large-scale industrial access networks.
